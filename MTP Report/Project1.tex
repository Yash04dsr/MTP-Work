\chapter*{Abstract}
The blending of hydrogen into existing high-pressure natural gas transmission pipelines (``Hydrogen-Enriched Natural Gas'' or HENG) offers a capital-efficient pathway for decarbonization. However, the distinct thermophysical properties of hydrogen--specifically its low density ($\approx 1/8^{\text{th}}$ of methane)--induce buoyancy-driven stratification, leading to safety risks such as localized hydrogen embrittlement and combustion instability.

Existing literature largely optimizes mixing at low pressures ($<10$ bar) or ignores the energy penalty associated with mixing devices. This study presents a high-fidelity Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) investigation at a realistic transmission pressure of \textbf{40 bar}, where compressibility and density disparities significantly alter the flow physics.

A unified parametric study is conducted on a T-junction configuration considering \textbf{two injection positions}--\textbf{Top Injection} and \textbf{Bottom Injection}--to comprehensively evaluate the role of buoyancy on mixing performance. To address the conflicting goals of maximizing homogeneity (Coefficient of Variation, $CoV$) and minimizing operational cost (Pressure Drop, $\Delta P$), a Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) framework is established. A Deep Neural Network (DNN) surrogate model is trained on CFD data and coupled with the NSGA-II genetic algorithm to identify the Pareto Optimal Frontier. The results provide industry-ready design guidelines for injection geometry (position, angle, and diameter ratio) that balance safety with economic viability.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}
The transition to a hydrogen economy is hindered by the prohibitive Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) required for dedicated infrastructure. Utilizing the existing natural gas grid via blending (Power-to-Gas) is a leading transitional strategy. However, the physical integration of hydrogen introduces profound fluid dynamic challenges.

At standard conditions, hydrogen ($\rho_{H_2} \approx 0.089$~kg/m$^3$) is significantly lighter than methane ($\rho_{CH_4} \approx 0.656$~kg/m$^3$). In pipeline flow, this density difference creates a Richardson Number ($Ri$) regime where buoyancy forces can dominate inertial mixing forces:
\begin{equation}
    Ri = \frac{g \, (\rho_{NG} - \rho_{H_2}) \, D}{\rho_{\text{avg}} \, v^2}
\end{equation}
If $Ri > 0.1$, hydrogen stratifies at the pipe crown (top). This exposes the steel to high partial pressures of hydrogen, accelerating \textbf{Hydrogen Embrittlement (HE)} and causing metering errors downstream. The injection position (top vs.\ bottom) fundamentally alters how buoyancy aids or opposes this stratification.

\section{Problem Statement}
The integration of hydrogen into natural gas networks presents a multi-physics optimization challenge characterized by two \textit{conflicting} objectives:

\subsection{The Conflict}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Maximizing Mixing Quality (Safety):} To prevent stratification and ensure a uniform Hydrogen Blending Ratio (HBR) at the outlet, high turbulence levels are required. This is typically achieved by high-velocity injection jets ($R_v \gg 1$), counter-flow angles, or turbulence promoters (static mixers). High mixing efficiency minimizes the Coefficient of Variation ($CoV$).
    \item \textbf{Minimizing Energy Loss (Cost):} High turbulence and jet impingement induce significant irreversible pressure losses ($\Delta P$) due to shear and eddy dissipation. This increases the compression power required at pumping stations, directly impacting the Operational Expenditure (OPEX).
\end{itemize}

Mathematically, this constitutes a \textbf{Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO)} problem. There is no single ``perfect'' solution; rather, there exists a set of trade-off solutions where improving one objective (e.g., lowering $CoV$) inevitably degrades the other (e.g., increasing $\Delta P$).

\subsection{Research Gap}
Existing studies \cite{Su2022, Arya2023} often focus on single-objective optimization (e.g., maximizing H$_2$ fraction) or operate at low distribution pressures ($<10$ bar). Furthermore, most studies consider only a single injection configuration. This study bridges the gap by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Modeling the process at transmission pressures ($P = 40$ bar), where real-gas effects are significant.
    \item Studying \textbf{both Top and Bottom injection} positions to quantify the role of buoyancy.
    \item Formulating a bi-objective problem ($CoV$ vs.\ $\Delta P$) to identify the Pareto optimal front.
    \item Using a Deep Learning surrogate to bypass the computational cost of direct CFD-in-the-loop optimization.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Theoretical Framework}

\section{Governing Physics}
The mixing process is governed by the compressible Navier-Stokes equations for a multi-component flow (non-reacting in this study, but species transport applies).

\subsection{Continuity Equation}
The steady-state continuity equation for the gas mixture is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \, \vec{v}) = 0
\end{equation}
For steady-state simulations this reduces to $\nabla \cdot (\rho \, \vec{v}) = 0$.

\subsection{Momentum Equation}
The momentum conservation equation, incorporating the gravitational body force $\vec{g}$ which drives buoyancy-induced stratification:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial (\rho \, \vec{v})}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \, \vec{v} \, \vec{v}) = -\nabla p + \nabla \cdot \left[ \mu \left( \nabla \vec{v} + (\nabla \vec{v})^T \right) \right] + \rho \, \vec{g}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the effective (molecular $+$ turbulent) dynamic viscosity and $p$ is the static pressure. At 40 bar, the gas density $\rho$ is calculated using the real-gas Equation of State (EOS) to account for compressibility effects ($Z \neq 1$).

\subsection{Species Transport Equation}
The transport of hydrogen mass fraction $Y_{H_2}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla \cdot (\rho \, \vec{v} \, Y_{H_2}) = -\nabla \cdot \vec{J}_{H_2}
\end{equation}
where $\vec{J}_{H_2}$ is the diffusion flux, modeled as:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{J}_{H_2} = -\left( \rho \, D_{m,H_2} + \frac{\mu_t}{Sc_t} \right) \nabla Y_{H_2}
\end{equation}
Here, $Sc_t$ is the turbulent Schmidt number (typically $\approx 0.7$). The turbulent viscosity $\mu_t$ dominates over molecular diffusion $D_{m,H_2}$ in high-Reynolds-number flows.

\subsection{Turbulence Model ($k$--$\varepsilon$)}
The standard $k$--$\varepsilon$ turbulence model \cite{Su2022} provides the turbulent kinetic energy $k$ and its dissipation rate $\varepsilon$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho k) + \nabla \cdot (\rho \, \vec{v} \, k) = \nabla \cdot \left[ \left( \mu + \frac{\mu_t}{\sigma_k} \right) \nabla k \right] + G_k + G_b - \rho \varepsilon - Y_M + S_k
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\rho \varepsilon) + \nabla \cdot (\rho \, \vec{v} \, \varepsilon) = \nabla \cdot \left[ \left( \mu + \frac{\mu_t}{\sigma_\varepsilon} \right) \nabla \varepsilon \right] + C_{1\varepsilon} \frac{\varepsilon}{k}(G_k + C_{3\varepsilon} G_b) - C_{2\varepsilon} \rho \frac{\varepsilon^2}{k} + S_\varepsilon
\end{equation}
where $G_k$ is the production of turbulent kinetic energy due to the mean velocity gradients, $G_b$ is that due to buoyancy, $Y_M$ is the contribution of fluctuating dilatation in compressible turbulence, and the model constants are $C_{1\varepsilon} = 1.44$, $C_{2\varepsilon} = 1.92$, $\sigma_k = 1.0$, $\sigma_\varepsilon = 1.3$.

\subsection{Quantifying Mixing Quality: Coefficient of Variation (CoV)}
Following Su et al.\ \cite{Su2022}, the Coefficient of Variation (CoV) is the metric used to quantify mixing homogeneity at a given pipeline cross-section:
\begin{equation}
    CoV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{c}} = \frac{1}{\bar{c}} \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (c_i - \bar{c})^2}
    \label{eq:cov}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $c_i$: hydrogen mole (or volume) fraction at sampling node $i$ on the cross-section,
    \item $\bar{c}$: area-weighted average hydrogen fraction across all $N$ sampling points,
    \item $N$: total number of grid nodes on the cross-section.
\end{itemize}
A value of $CoV < 0.05$ is generally accepted as ``uniformly mixed'' in engineering practice. A $CoV$ of $0$ corresponds to perfect homogeneity, while $CoV \to 1$ indicates severe stratification.

\section{Pareto Optimization Theory}
In single-objective optimization, we seek a global minimum $x^*$ such that $f(x^*) \leq f(x)$ for all feasible $x$. In Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO), no single point simultaneously minimizes all objectives; instead, we seek a set of solutions called the \textbf{Pareto Frontier}.

\subsection{Dominance}
A solution $\mathbf{x}_1$ is said to \textit{dominate} $\mathbf{x}_2$ (written $\mathbf{x}_1 \succ \mathbf{x}_2$) if and only if:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $f_k(\mathbf{x}_1) \leq f_k(\mathbf{x}_2)$ for all objectives $k = 1, 2, \ldots, m$ \quad (\textit{no worse in any objective}),
    \item $\exists\; j$ such that $f_j(\mathbf{x}_1) < f_j(\mathbf{x}_2)$ \quad (\textit{strictly better in at least one}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Pareto Optimality}
A solution $\mathbf{x}^*$ is \textbf{Pareto Optimal} if there exists no other feasible solution $\mathbf{x}$ that dominates it. The set of all Pareto optimal solutions in the objective space forms the \textbf{Pareto Front}.

\subsection{Variable Classification}
In the context of this study, we classify the variables as follows:

\noindent The fundamental trade-off is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We cannot minimize $\Delta P$ without eventually increasing $CoV$ (worsening mixing).
    \item We cannot minimize $CoV$ (improve mixing) without increasing $\Delta P$ (higher energy cost).
\end{itemize}
The \textbf{Pareto Front} is the curve connecting all non-dominated solutions. The ``Knee Point'' on this curve typically represents the optimal engineering trade-off where marginal improvement in one objective causes a disproportionate degradation in the other.

\chapter{Computational Methodology}

\section{Geometric Configuration}
The study considers a T-junction pipeline where hydrogen is injected into a main natural gas pipeline through a branch pipe. Two distinct injection positions are investigated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bottom Injection} ($y = -D/2$): The light gas (hydrogen) is injected from the bottom of the pipe. Buoyancy forces cause the hydrogen to rise through the heavier methane, naturally promoting vertical mixing.
    \item \textbf{Top Injection} ($y = +D/2$): Hydrogen is injected from the top of the pipe. Here, buoyancy opposes dispersion--the already-light hydrogen tends to remain stratified at the crown. This configuration tests the ``worst-case'' scenario and quantifies the penalty of unfavorable buoyancy.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Comparing these two configurations is essential because real pipeline networks may have injection points at various orientations. The parametric study covers both to provide general design guidance.

\section{Solver Framework}
The mathematical model defined in Section~2 is solved using the \textbf{Finite Volume Method (FVM)}. The simulation resolves the steady-state, compressible Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations coupled with species transport.

\subsection{Turbulence Modeling}
The \textbf{Realizable $k$--$\varepsilon$} model is selected over the Standard $k$--$\varepsilon$ model.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Justification:} The hydrogen jet creates strong streamline curvature and anisotropic turbulence at the injection point. The Realizable variant contains a modified formulation for the turbulent viscosity ($C_\mu$ is variable rather than constant), providing higher accuracy for Jet-in-Crossflow (JICF) physics and jet spreading rates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Equation of State (The 40 bar Correction)}
Standard ``Incompressible Ideal Gas'' assumptions are invalid at 40 bar.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Equation of State (EOS):} \textbf{Peng-Robinson} or \textbf{Redlich-Kwong}.
    \item \textbf{Justification:} These real-gas models accurately predict the density ($\rho$) and compressibility factor ($Z$) of the H$_2$--CH$_4$ mixture at high pressures, which is critical for capturing the correct buoyancy forces ($\Delta \rho \approx 20$~kg/m$^3$ at 40 bar).
\end{itemize}

\section{Numerical Setup}

\subsection{Domain and Mesh}
\begin{itemize}

\chapter{Multi-Objective Optimization Framework}

\section{Problem Formulation}
The design of the injection system is a trade-off between safety (mixing quality) and cost (pressure drop). We define the optimization problem as:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Minimize } \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} f_1(\mathbf{x}) \\ f_2(\mathbf{x}) \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\noindent \textbf{Objectives:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $f_1 = CoV$: Coefficient of Variation measured at $L = 50D$ downstream. Target: $CoV < 0.05$.
    \item $f_2 = \Delta P$: Total pressure drop from inlet to outlet. Target: Minimize.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{Subject to constraints:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $5\% \leq HBR \leq 30\%$ (safe blending range per international standards),
    \item $P_{\text{outlet}} = 40$~bar (fixed operating pressure),
    \item $P_{\text{inj}} \in \{\text{Top}, \text{Bottom}\}$ (both configurations are evaluated).
\end{itemize}

\section{Workflow Implementation}

\subsection{Step 1: Design of Experiments (DoE)}
A well-structured Design of Experiments is critical to ensure that the surrogate model is trained on a representative and space-filling dataset. In this study, the four design variables are partitioned into two categories based on their physical role in the CFD workflow:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Geometry Variables} ($\theta$, $\beta$): These define the physical shape of the T-junction and therefore require a \textit{new mesh} for each combination. They are treated as \textbf{discrete} variables sampled via Full Factorial design.
    \item \textbf{Boundary Condition Variables} ($R_v$, $HBR$): These affect only the inlet velocities and mass fractions, and can be changed parametrically \textit{within the same mesh}. They are treated as \textbf{continuous} variables sampled via optimized Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS).
\end{itemize}

\noindent This hybrid Discrete $\times$ LHS strategy is both computationally efficient (only 15 unique meshes need to be constructed) and statistically rigorous (each mesh block has a space-filling continuous sub-design).

\textbf{Construction Procedure:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Discrete Factorial:} Define $5$ injection angles $\theta \in \{30^\circ, 60^\circ, 90^\circ, 120^\circ, 150^\circ\}$ and $3$ diameter ratios $\beta \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5\}$. This yields $5 \times 3 = \textbf{15}$ unique mesh geometries.
    \item \textbf{Per-Mesh LHS:} For each of the 15 meshes, generate $N = 14$ optimized LHS samples in the 2-dimensional continuous space $[R_v, HBR]$ using the Centered $L_2$-star Discrepancy criterion.
    \item \textbf{Injection Position Replication:} Replicate the entire matrix for both Top and Bottom injection (gravity vector change only; no re-meshing required).
    \item \textbf{Total:} $15 \text{ meshes} \times 14 \text{ LHS samples} \times 2 \text{ positions} = \textbf{420}$ CFD runs.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Step 2: Surrogate Modeling (Deep Neural Network)}
Running CFD inside an optimization loop is computationally prohibitive (each simulation takes $\sim$4--8 hours). A \textbf{Deep Neural Network (DNN)} surrogate model is trained to approximate the CFD input--output relationship at negligible cost ($\sim$1~ms per evaluation).

\subsubsection{Input Encoding and Preprocessing}
The five design variables are encoded into a 6-dimensional feature vector:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x} = [\underbrace{x_1, x_2}_{\text{One-Hot}(P_{\text{inj}})},\; \underbrace{x_3}_{\theta},\; \underbrace{x_4}_{\beta},\; \underbrace{x_5}_{R_v},\; \underbrace{x_6}_{HBR}] \in \mathbb{R}^{6}
\end{equation}
where the categorical injection position is one-hot encoded as:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{inj}} = \begin{cases}
        [1,\, 0] & \text{if Top injection} \\
        [0,\, 1] & \text{if Bottom injection}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
All features are MinMax-normalized to $[0, 1]$ using the training set statistics:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{x}_i = \frac{x_i - x_i^{\min}}{x_i^{\max} - x_i^{\min}}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, 6
\end{equation}
The target vector $\mathbf{y} = [CoV,\; \Delta P]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$ is similarly normalized.

\subsubsection{Network Architecture}
The DNN is a fully connected feedforward network with four hidden layers, designed with an \textbf{expanding-then-contracting} topology:

\begin{equation}
    \underset{(6)}{\text{Input}} 
    \xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_1, \mathbf{b}_1}
    \underset{(64)}{\text{H}_1}
    \xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_2, \mathbf{b}_2}
    \underset{(128)}{\text{H}_2}
    \xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_3, \mathbf{b}_3}
    \underset{(64)}{\text{H}_3}
    \xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_4, \mathbf{b}_4}
    \underset{(32)}{\text{H}_4}
    \xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_5, \mathbf{b}_5}
    \underset{(2)}{\text{Output}}
\end{equation}

\noindent The rationale behind this architecture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 1 ($64$ neurons):} Lifts the 6D input to a higher-dimensional feature space to capture pairwise variable interactions.
    \item \textbf{Layer 2 ($128$ neurons):} The widest layer captures complex non-linear cross-effects (e.g., the interaction between $\theta$ and $R_v$ on turbulent mixing).
    \item \textbf{Layers 3--4 ($64 \to 32$ neurons):} Progressive compression distills the learned features into the most relevant representations for prediction.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The forward pass through hidden layer $l$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}^{(l)} = \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{W}^{(l)} \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(l)}\right), \quad l = 1, \ldots, 4
    \label{eq:forward}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the \textbf{ReLU} (Rectified Linear Unit) activation:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(z) = \max(0,\, z)
\end{equation}
The output layer uses a \textbf{linear} (identity) activation to allow unrestricted prediction of physical quantities:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{W}^{(5)} \mathbf{h}^{(4)} + \mathbf{b}^{(5)} \in \mathbb{R}^{2}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Loss Function and Optimization}
The model is trained to minimize the \textbf{Mean Squared Error (MSE)} loss with $L_2$ weight regularization:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N_{\text{train}}} \sum_{k=1}^{N_{\text{train}}} \| \hat{\mathbf{y}}_k - \mathbf{y}_k \|_2^2 \;+\; \alpha \sum_{l=1}^{5} \| \mathbf{W}^{(l)} \|_F^2
    \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 10^{-4}$ is the $L_2$ regularization coefficient and $\| \cdot \|_F$ denotes the Frobenius norm. The regularization term penalizes large weights, preventing the network from overfitting to the 420 training samples.

\subsection{Step 3: Evolutionary Optimization (NSGA-II)}
The \textbf{Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II)} is employed to solve the bi-objective optimization problem using the trained DNN surrogate as a fast evaluator ($\sim$1~ms vs $\sim$6 hours per CFD evaluation).

\subsubsection{Formal Problem Statement}
The multi-objective optimization problem is formally stated as:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}} \; \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} \widehat{CoV}(\mathbf{x}) \\ \widehat{\Delta P}(\mathbf{x}) \end{bmatrix}
    \label{eq:moo}
\end{equation}
subject to the design variable bounds:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{X} = \left\{ \mathbf{x} \;\middle|\; \theta \in [30^\circ, 150^\circ],\; \beta \in [0.1, 0.5],\; R_v \in [0.5, 4.0],\; HBR \in [0.05, 0.30] \right\}
\end{equation}
\noindent where $\widehat{CoV}$ and $\widehat{\Delta P}$ are the DNN surrogate predictions. The optimization is run independently for each injection position ($P_{\text{inj}} \in \{\text{Top}, \text{Bottom}\}$), producing two overlapping Pareto fronts.

\subsubsection{Algorithm Configuration}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initialization:} $N_{\text{pop}} = 200$ random designs sampled uniformly within $\mathcal{X}$.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation:} Query the DNN surrogate for $[\widehat{CoV},\; \widehat{\Delta P}]$ (0.001~s per design).
    \item \textbf{Selection:} Rank designs via \textit{non-dominated sorting} (Pareto frontiers $\mathcal{F}_1, \mathcal{F}_2, \ldots$) with \textit{crowding distance} as the secondary criterion to maintain diversity along the Pareto front.
    \item \textbf{Crossover:} \textbf{Simulated Binary Crossover (SBX)} with distribution index $\eta_c = 15$ and probability $p_c = 0.9$. A high $\eta_c$ biases offspring close to parents, exploiting promising regions.
    \item \textbf{Mutation:} \textbf{Polynomial Mutation (PM)} with distribution index $\eta_m = 20$. Provides controlled exploration of the design space.
    \item \textbf{Termination:} $G = 150$ generations. Total DNN evaluations: $N_{\text{pop}} \times G = 30{,}000$ (completes in $<$30~seconds).
    \item \textbf{Result:} A Pareto Frontier $\mathcal{P}^*$ representing the set of Pareto-optimal trade-off designs between mixing quality and hydraulic efficiency.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The key advantage of this approach is that the entire optimization--which would require $30{,}000 \times 6$~hours $\approx$ \textbf{20 years} of CFD time--is completed in under 30~seconds using the DNN surrogate.

\chapter{Progress Report: Design of Experiments Generation}

This section documents the computational generation and statistical validation of the 420-case hybrid Design of Experiments (DoE) matrix described in Section~4.2.1. The DoE was implemented in Python using SciPy's Quasi-Monte Carlo framework (\texttt{scipy.stats.qmc}) and is the foundational dataset upon which the entire surrogate-assisted optimization pipeline is built.

The key innovation in this DoE is the \textbf{hybrid discrete--continuous} sampling strategy. The geometry variables ($\theta$, $\beta$) are treated as discrete factors (since each unique combination requires a separate CFD mesh), while the boundary condition variables ($R_v$, $HBR$) are sampled continuously within each mesh block using optimized LHS. This approach is both computationally practical (only 15 unique meshes need to be constructed) and statistically rigorous (each mesh block has a space-filling sub-design in the continuous variable space).

\section{Latin Hypercube Sampling: Algorithm and Optimality}

\subsection{Why LHS over Alternatives}
Several sampling strategies exist for computer experiments. Table~\ref{tab:lhs_comparison} provides a rigorous comparison for sampling the continuous boundary condition space ($R_v$, $HBR$) within each mesh block.

\subsection{Standard Latin Hypercube Construction}
For $N = 14$ samples in $d = 2$ dimensions (applied independently within each of the 15 mesh blocks), the LHS algorithm proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Stratification:} Divide each variable's $[0, 1]$ range into $N = 14$ equal strata of width $1/N \approx 0.071$.
    \item \textbf{Sampling:} In each stratum $j$ of variable $i$, draw one random value:
    \begin{equation}
        u_{ij} = \frac{\pi_{i}(j) - 1 + r_{ij}}{N}, \quad r_{ij} \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)
    \end{equation}
    where $\pi_i$ is a random permutation of $\{1, 2, \ldots, N\}$ for dimension $i$.
    \item \textbf{Result:} A sample matrix $\mathbf{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ where each column is a permutation of strata--guaranteeing that the marginal distribution of each variable is exactly uniform.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent This construction ensures \textit{marginal stratification}: when projected onto any single axis, the $N$ points cover all $N$ strata exactly once. However, in the joint $d$-dimensional space, points may still cluster or leave gaps.

\subsection{Centered Discrepancy Optimization}
To achieve global space-filling uniformity (not just marginal uniformity), we optimize the LHS using the \textbf{Centered $L_2$-Star Discrepancy} ($CD$) criterion. The $CD$ measures the maximum deviation of the empirical distribution of sample points from the uniform distribution over the entire hypercube:

\begin{equation}
    CD^2(\mathbf{U}) = \left(\frac{13}{12}\right)^d - \frac{2}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \prod_{i=1}^{d} \left(1 + \frac{1}{2}|u_{ki} - 0.5| - \frac{1}{2}|u_{ki} - 0.5|^2 \right) + \frac{1}{N^2} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \prod_{i=1}^{d} g(u_{ki}, u_{ji})
    \label{eq:cd}
\end{equation}
where $g(a, b) = 1 + \frac{1}{2}|a - 0.5| + \frac{1}{2}|b - 0.5| - \frac{1}{2}|a - b|$.

\noindent The optimization algorithm generates multiple random LHS permutations and selects the one achieving the lowest $CD$ value--a stochastic search over the combinatorial space of column permutations. Lower discrepancy $\implies$ more uniform space-filling.

\subsection{Scaling to Physical Ranges}
After generating the optimal LHS in the unit hypercube $[0,1]^2$, each variable is linearly scaled to its physical range:
\begin{equation}
    x_i = x_i^{\min} + u_i \cdot (x_i^{\max} - x_i^{\min})
\end{equation}
Specifically, $R_v$ is scaled from $[0,1]$ to $[0.5, 4.0]$ and $HBR$ from $[0,1]$ to $[0.05, 0.30]$.

\subsection{Geometry Variables: Discrete Full Factorial}
The geometry variables $\theta \in \{30^\circ, 60^\circ, 90^\circ, 120^\circ, 150^\circ\}$ and $\beta \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5\}$ are treated as a full factorial design, yielding $5 \times 3 = 15$ unique mesh geometries. Each mesh is named systematically (e.g., \texttt{Mesh\_T90\_B0.3}) to facilitate automated batch simulation. The injection position $P_{\text{inj}} \in \{\text{Top}, \text{Bottom}\}$ is handled by inverting the gravity vector--no re-meshing is required--and the 210-case matrix is replicated identically for both positions.

\subsection{$\Delta P$ Calibration (Darcy-Weisbach / Idelchik~\cite{Idelchik1994})}

The pressure drop model is based on the classical Darcy-Weisbach minor loss formulation for T-junction branch flow~\cite{Idelchik1994}:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta P = K \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} \rho_{NG}\, v_{\text{jet}}^2
    \label{eq:dp_syn}
\end{equation}
where the minor loss coefficient $K$ depends on geometry:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $K_{90^\circ} \approx 1.0$--$1.8$ for branch flow (Idelchik, Ch.~7~\cite{Idelchik1994})
    \item $K$ scales with $\sin(\theta)$ for oblique junctions
    \item $K$ increases with decreasing $\beta$ (flow restriction)
\end{itemize}
\noindent Using $\rho_{NG} = 28.7$~kg/m$^3$ at 4~MPa/300~K (computed from the Peng-Robinson EOS in the DoE generator) and $v_{\text{main}} = 5$~m/s (typical transmission velocity), the dynamic pressure is $q = \frac{1}{2}(28.7)(R_v \times 5)^2 = 358.75\,R_v^2$~Pa.

\noindent Gaussian noise ($\sigma_{CoV} = 0.005$, $\sigma_{\Delta P} = 150$~Pa) was added to simulate CFD variability. The resulting synthetic data spans: $CoV \in [0.07, 0.48]$ and $\Delta P \in [50, 33{,}894]$~Pa.

\noindent \textbf{Interpretation:} The synthetic formulas reproduce the correct \textit{order of magnitude} and \textit{parametric trends} observed in literature:
\begin{itemize}
    \item CoV ranges from $\sim$0.05 (well-mixed) to $\sim$0.50 (stratified), matching Su et al.'s reported range of 0.049--0.673.
    \item $\Delta P$ ranges from $\sim$50~Pa (low velocity, co-flow) to $\sim$34{,}000~Pa (high velocity, counter-flow), consistent with T-junction minor loss physics for 40-bar natural gas.
    \item Deviations of $\pm$15--26\% at specific operating points are \textit{expected} because the multiplicative model neglects variable interaction effects (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Known Limitations and Assumptions}
\label{sec:limitations}

The synthetic data model makes several simplifying assumptions that introduce systematic deviations from real CFD data. Table~\ref{tab:limitations} provides a complete inventory:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Known limitations of the synthetic response formulas}
\label{tab:limitations}
\begin{tabular}{@{}clp{7.5cm}c@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{\#} & \textbf{Assumption} & \textbf{Impact on Fidelity} & \textbf{Severity} \\ \midrule
1 & Multiplicative independence & Variables are combined as independent factors. In reality, $\theta$ and $R_v$ interact strongly --- a counter-flow jet at high $R_v$ produces a qualitatively different flow structure (recirculation zone) than the product of individual effects suggests. & High \\
2 & Power-law velocity scaling & $CoV \propto R_v^{0.8}$ assumes monotonic increase. CFD studies show \textbf{saturation} at $R_v > 3$--$4$ where the jet over-penetrates and CoV plateaus or even increases (diminishing returns). & Medium \\
3 & No turbulence model physics & The formulas capture mean trends but not turbulent fluctuation effects. Real CFD with the $k$--$\varepsilon$ model would capture shear-layer vortices and their effect on mixing. & High \\
4 & Linear buoyancy penalty & The 20\% CoV penalty for Top injection is constant; in reality, buoyancy effects depend on $Ri$ (Richardson number), which varies with $R_v$ and $\beta$. & Medium \\
5 & Steady-state single-phase & The synthetic model ignores transient effects (vortex shedding, oscillating jets) that real CFD might reveal, especially at high $R_v$. & Low--Medium \\
6 & Noise model ($\sigma$ = const.) & Gaussian noise with fixed $\sigma$ is added; real CFD variability depends on mesh sensitivity, convergence criteria, and operating point. & Low \\
7 & No 3D geometry effects & The formulas assume idealized T-junction geometry. Real pipes have upstream fittings, roughness variations, and possible misalignment that affect mixing. & Low \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Overall Assessment}

\noindent In summary, the synthetic data formulas are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Adequate for pipeline validation:} The multiplicative model preserves the \textit{correct ranking} of variable importance ($R_v > \theta > \beta > HBR > P_{\text{inj}}$), produces \textit{physically plausible} response ranges, and generates data with sufficient nonlinearity for the DNN to learn meaningful mappings. The DNN architecture, training procedure, and NSGA-II optimization can be fully validated without waiting for CFD data.
    \item \textbf{Inadequate for final conclusions:} The $\pm$15--26\% deviations at specific points, absence of variable interactions (e.g., $\theta \times R_v$), and lack of turbulence physics mean that the Pareto front generated from synthetic data should \textbf{not} be used for design recommendations. The final Pareto front \textit{must} be based on real CFD data.
    \item \textbf{Expected improvement with real data:} When CFD results replace the synthetic responses, we expect (i) the DNN $R^2$ to change (potentially decrease initially due to CFD noise, but improve with hyperparameter tuning), (ii) the Pareto front shape to shift, and (iii) new interaction effects (e.g., jet recirculation at high $\theta \times R_v$) to emerge.
\end{itemize}



The DNN surrogate (architecture described in Section~4.2.2) was trained on 336 samples (80\% of 420) with 84 samples reserved for testing. Training converged in \textbf{220 epochs} (out of a maximum 1{,}000), terminating via early stopping.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Clear Trade-Off:} As $CoV$ decreases (better mixing), $\Delta P$ increases sharply--confirming the fundamental conflict between mixing quality and hydraulic efficiency identified in Section~2.2.
    \item \textbf{Bottom Injection Dominance:} The Bottom injection Pareto front consistently lies below (lower $\Delta P$) or to the left (lower $CoV$) of the Top injection front, as predicted by the buoyancy hypothesis (Section~5.1). This validates the design of the T-junction study.
    \item \textbf{Region Identification:} Three regions are visible:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Region A (Low Cost):} High $CoV$ ($> 0.15$), low $\Delta P$ -- poor mixing, unsafe.
        \item \textbf{Region B (High Performance):} Very low $CoV$ ($< 0.05$), but high $\Delta P$ ($> 15{,}000$~Pa) -- excellent mixing but higher hydraulic cost.
        \item \textbf{Knee Region:} The optimal engineering compromise zone, where moderate increases in $\Delta P$ yield large improvements in $CoV$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Future Plan}

The surrogate-assisted optimization pipeline has been fully assembled and validated on literature-calibrated synthetic data. The remaining work is structured into three phases, each building on the outputs of the previous one.

\section{Phase 1: CFD Simulations}

The 420 cases from the hybrid DoE (Section~4.2.1) will be executed in ANSYS Fluent using the simulation settings described in Section~3.4. Key activities include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mesh generation:} Construct 15 unique T-junction meshes (5 angles $\times$ 3 diameter ratios) with structured hexahedral cells and boundary-layer refinement near the injection nozzle.
    \item \textbf{Batch simulation:} Automate the 420 CFD runs using journal files and a Python scheduler. Each case solves the steady-state RANS equations (standard $k$-$\varepsilon$) with species transport for the H$_2$-CH$_4$ binary mixture.
    \item \textbf{Post-processing:} Extract $CoV$ (on a cross-section at $z = 40D$ downstream) and $\Delta P$ (between inlet and outlet) from each converged solution.
    \item \textbf{Estimated compute time:} $\sim$2--3 hours per case $\times$ 420 cases $\approx$ 1050 core-hours (parallelized across available HPC nodes).
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 2: Surrogate Training on Real Data}

Once CFD data is available, the pipeline (\texttt{surrogate\_pipeline.py}) will be re-executed with the real $CoV$ and $\Delta P$ values. The DNN architecture (Section~4.2.2) will be retrained, with potential hyperparameter tuning:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Evaluate alternative architectures (e.g., wider networks, residual connections) if $R^2 < 0.95$ on test data.
    \item Compare DNN against Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) and Gradient Boosting as baseline surrogates.
    \item Perform $k$-fold cross-validation ($k = 5$) for robust accuracy estimation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 3: Optimization and Pareto Analysis}

The validated surrogate will be coupled with NSGA-II (Section~4.2.3) to generate the final Pareto frontier:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Extract the Pareto-optimal design set for both Top and Bottom injection.
    \item Identify the ``knee'' region using the TOPSIS multi-criteria decision method.
    \item Select 3--5 representative designs from the Pareto front and verify them with independent CFD simulations (not used in training) to confirm surrogate fidelity.
    \item Perform sensitivity analysis using SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) to rank variable importance.
\end{itemize}
