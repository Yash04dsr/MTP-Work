\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

This guide documents a \emph{reference} OpenFOAM setup that reproduces
the transient DDES simulation of hydrogen injection into a
methane-filled T-junction reported by
\textbf{Eames, Austin \& Wojcik} (\emph{International Journal of
Hydrogen Energy}, 47:25745--25754, 2022).  Every dictionary file,
boundary condition and solver setting has been chosen to match
either the paper directly or the companion Fluent configuration
(where the Fluent and OpenFOAM setups are intended to be
numerically equivalent).

\section{Physical Problem}

Pure hydrogen is injected at high pressure into a methane natural-gas
pipeline through a $90^{\circ}$ T-junction.  Two aspects make the
flow unusual:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The density ratio $\rho_{\mathrm{CH_4}}/\rho_{\mathrm{H_2}}
        \approx 8$ at 6.9\,MPa --- far larger than typical single-phase
        flows --- generates strong \textbf{buoyancy-driven
        stratification}.
  \item The low inertia of the H$_2$ branch stream leads to weak jet
        penetration, concentrating hydrogen against the pipe wall
        rather than mixing into the bulk.
\end{enumerate}

The engineering consequence is \textbf{hydrogen embrittlement}:
localised, near-wall H$_2$ concentrations much higher than the
nominal blend fraction can accelerate fatigue crack growth in high
tensile carbon steels.

\section{Objectives of the Simulation}

Following the paper, the simulation must resolve:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{time-averaged wall H$_2$ volume fraction}
        $\overline{V_R}$ as a function of downstream distance
        $z/d_1$, for multiple dilution factors.
  \item The \textbf{maximum vertical-plane H$_2$ concentration}
        $V_{Rm}$ and its decay with $z$.
  \item The \textbf{stratification asymmetry} between the upper and
        lower pipe walls.
  \item Ultimately, the ratio
        $\mathcal{R} = \overline{V_{Rm}}/V_{R,T}$
        at $z/d_1 = 8$, which the paper shows varies from 4.4 to 1.6
        across the blend-fraction targets of 5--20\,\%.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\fbox{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{Why this solver?}  Eames et al.\ use
\texttt{rhoReactingBuoyantFoam} \emph{specifically} because it
solves for $p_{\mathrm{rgh}} = p - \rho\,\mathbf{g}\!\cdot\!\mathbf{h}$
rather than $p$ directly.  Removing the hydrostatic component from
the momentum equation is essential for a flow where buoyancy is the
dominant driver of the key physical result (stratification).  Using
the simpler \texttt{reactingFoam} would introduce spurious numerical
noise in the pressure field and degrade the stratification
prediction.}}

\chapter{Governing Equations}
\label{ch:goveq}

These are the equations actually solved by
\texttt{rhoReactingBuoyantFoam}, identical to those of the paper.

\section{Continuity}

\[
  \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}
  + \nabla \!\cdot\! (\rho \mathbf{u}) = 0
\]

\section{Momentum (with Buoyant Pressure Decomposition)}

The solver writes $p = p_{\mathrm{rgh}} + \rho\,\mathbf{g}\!\cdot\!
\mathbf{h}$ and advances
$p_{\mathrm{rgh}}$ instead of $p$:

\[
  \frac{\partial (\rho u_i)}{\partial t}
  + \frac{\partial (\rho u_i u_j)}{\partial x_j}
  = -\frac{\partial p_{\mathrm{rgh}}}{\partial x_i}
    - (\mathbf{g}\!\cdot\!\mathbf{h}) \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x_i}
    + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\!
      \left[\mu_e\!\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}
      + \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}\right)\right]
    + \rho g_i
\]

\noindent The second term on the right absorbs the hydrostatic
gradient, so $p_{\mathrm{rgh}}$ has only the dynamic contribution.

\section{Species Transport}

For each species $k \in \{\mathrm{H_2},\mathrm{CH_4}\}$:

\[
  \frac{\partial (\rho Y_k)}{\partial t}
  + \nabla\!\cdot\!(\rho Y_k \mathbf{u})
  = \nabla\!\cdot\!(\mu_e \nabla Y_k)
\]

\noindent CH$_4$ is the \textbf{inert (balance) species}, so
$Y_{\mathrm{CH_4}} = 1 - Y_{\mathrm{H_2}}$ everywhere.

\section{Sensible Enthalpy}

\[
  \frac{\partial (\rho h)}{\partial t}
  + \nabla\!\cdot\!(\rho \mathbf{u} h)
  + \frac{\partial (\rho K)}{\partial t}
  + \nabla\!\cdot\!(\rho \mathbf{u} K)
  - \frac{\partial p}{\partial t}
  = \nabla\!\cdot\!(\mu_e \nabla h)
\]

\noindent with $K = \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{u}|^2$ the specific kinetic
energy.  This matches equation (11) of the paper verbatim.

\section{Closure: Turbulence Model}

The modified turbulent viscosity $\tilde{\nu}$ obeys the
Spalart--Allmaras equation with the DDES length-scale modification,
closed by \texttt{SpalartAllmarasDDES} in OpenFOAM.

\section{Power-Law Inlet Velocity Profile}

Eames et al.\ impose a fully-developed turbulent pipe profile at
both inlets:

\begin{equation}
  u_r = u^{\!*}\left[1 - \left(\frac{2r}{d^{\!*}}\right)^{\!2}\right]^{1/n},
  \qquad n = 7
  \label{eq:powerlaw}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $r$ is the radial distance from the pipe centreline
and $d^{\!*}$, $u^{\!*}$ are the pipe diameter and centreline speed.
In OpenFOAM this is implemented with a \texttt{codedFixedValue} BC
whose kernel is compiled at the first run:

\begin{lstlisting}[language={},title=0/U (main\_inlet excerpt)]
main_inlet
{
    type    codedFixedValue;
    value   uniform (0 0 10);
    name    mainInletPowerLaw;
    code
    #{
        const vectorField& Cf = this->patch().Cf();
        vectorField& field = *this;
        const scalar d    = 0.46,  uMax = 10.0,  n = 7.0;
        forAll(Cf, i)
        {
            const scalar r   = Foam::sqrt(sqr(Cf[i].x())
                                        + sqr(Cf[i].y()));
            const scalar arg = Foam::max(1.0 - sqr(2*r/d), scalar(0));
            field[i] = vector(0, 0, uMax*Foam::pow(arg, 1.0/n));
        }
    #};
}
\end{lstlisting}

The branch inlet uses the same formula with
$d^{\!*} = 0.115$\,m, $u^{\!*} = 32$\,m/s along $-Y$, and radius
measured from the branch axis (which passes through
$(0,\,y,\,4.6)$).

\subsection{Branch Velocity Derivation}

Using the paper's definition of the volumetric dilution factor:
\[
  \mathcal{D}
  = \left(\frac{d_2}{d_1}\right)^{\!2} \frac{u_2}{u_1}
  = (0.25)^2 \frac{u_2}{10}
\]
For $\mathcal{D} = 0.2$ this gives $u_2 = 32$\,m/s, matching
equations (2)--(7) of the paper.

\textbf{Why LUST?}  LUST blends 75\,\% linear central difference
with 25\,\% linearUpwind.  It is the low-dissipation scheme that
OpenFOAM developers specifically recommend for LES/DES momentum
convection, and is the closest equivalent to Fluent's Bounded
Central Differencing.}}

\subsection{Hydrogen Volume Fraction (Coded Field)}

A \texttt{coded} function object computes and writes $\phi_{H_2}$
at every write step:

\begin{equation}
  \phi_{H_2}
  = \frac{Y_{H_2}/M_{H_2}}
         {Y_{H_2}/M_{H_2} + (1-Y_{H_2})/M_{CH_4}}
  \qquad (M \text{ in kg/kmol})
\end{equation}

\begin{lstlisting}[language={},numbers=none]
phiH2
{
    type  coded;
    name  phiH2Compute;
    writeControl writeTime;
    codeExecute
    #{
        const volScalarField& Y_H2 =
            mesh().lookupObject<volScalarField>("H2");
        volScalarField phiH2( ..., (Y_H2/2.016)
              / (Y_H2/2.016 + (1.0-Y_H2)/16.043) );
        phiH2.write();
    #};
}
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Validation Strategy}

Eames et al.\ validate their OpenFOAM setup against the
\textbf{OECD/NEA--V\"attenfall T-junction benchmark}~\cite{Braillard2018},
characterised by $Q_1/Q_2 = 1,\ d_1/d_2 = 1$ and a matched-Froude
cold/hot water mixing experiment.

For consistency, the same benchmark should be used for OpenFOAM
validation here:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Rebuild the case with \textbf{isothermal water-like
        properties} using the same T-junction geometry but at
        $d_1/d_2 = 1$ and $Q_1/Q_2 = 1$.
  \item Match the \textbf{Froude number}
        $Fr = u_2 / \sqrt{|\rho_1-\rho_2|\,g/\rho_1}$
        reported in~\cite{Braillard2018}.
  \item Compare the mean and RMS streamwise velocity in the
        centreline vertical plane at $z = 1.86\,d_1$ to experimental
        profiles.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Paper Fig.\ 2(b) confirms good agreement at
2\,M cells; finer meshes are unnecessary.

\section{Paper Physical Findings to Reproduce}

The simulation should, qualitatively and quantitatively, reveal:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Stratification.}  For $\mathcal{D} < 0.3$, H$_2$
        is confined to the upper portion of the pipe by buoyancy.
        Top-wall H$_2$ concentration $>$ 40\,\% for up to
        $8\,d_1$ downstream.
  \item \textbf{Weak jet penetration.}  The low-inertia H$_2$ jet
        is deflected by the main flow and fails to traverse the
        main pipe for $\mathcal{D} \lesssim 0.2$.
  \item \textbf{Periodic shedding.}  At $\mathcal{D} = 0.1$ puffs
        separated by $\sim 0.8$\,m ($St \sim 0.2$).
  \item \textbf{Asymmetric wall exposure.}  The lower pipe wall
        sees much less H$_2$ than the upper wall for
        $\mathcal{D} \leq 0.2$.  This is the stratification signal
        that should appear clearly in the wall-probe time series.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Coefficient of Variation (COV)}

COV quantifies mixing uniformity on a cross-section:
\[
  \text{COV} = \frac{\sigma_{\phi}}{\bar{\phi}},
  \quad
  \sigma_{\phi} = \sqrt{\texttt{H2Prime2Mean}},
  \quad
  \bar{\phi} = \texttt{H2Mean}.
\]

\noindent $\text{COV} = 0$ denotes perfect mixing,
$\text{COV} = 1$ denotes complete segregation.


\section{Mesh Independence (GCI)}

Per the paper, run three mesh refinement levels
(1.0, 2.0, 4.0\,M cells) and compute the \textbf{Grid Convergence
Index} (Roache 1994) on the converged outlet $V_R$:
\[
  p = \frac{\ln\!\left|\tfrac{\phi_3 - \phi_2}{\phi_2 - \phi_1}\right|}
           {\ln r},
  \qquad
  \text{GCI}_{\text{fine}}
  = F_s \frac{|(\phi_2-\phi_1)/\phi_1|}{r^p - 1},
  \quad F_s = 1.25.
\]

\noindent A GCI $< 2\,\%$ at the finest level confirms mesh
independence.
